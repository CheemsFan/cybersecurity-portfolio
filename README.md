## About Me

I got into cybersecurity through the SOC door. My first role was at an MSSP monitoring 
alerts, triaging incidents, and learning what real attacks look like when they hit a 
mid-market company with a small security team and no room for error. Six years later I

intelligence program from scratch, and managed security operations across cloud environments 
on three continents.

I am now focused on moving into the engineering side of security — specifically DevSecOps, 
cloud security, and application security. I enrolled in the SANS Technology Institute B.S. 
program to build that depth formally, and competing in NCL has been a good gut check on 
where my skills actually are versus where I think they are.

## CTF Reflection

Network traffic analysis and reconnaissance are areas I use at work, so I went in expecting 
to do well there. The web application exploitation results were a pleasant surprise. I have 
been working through OWASP Juice Shop on my own and I think that practice showed up directly 
in competition. Finishing all three web exploitation challenges with 100% accuracy felt good.

The hard-tier cryptography challenge was a clean miss. AES-CBC bit flipping is something I 
understand conceptually but I ran out of time actually implementing it under pressure. That 
gap is on my radar. Same with the log analysis hard challenge — cross-referencing JSON logs 
against threat intelligence under a time limit exposed some inefficiency in how I approach 
that kind of problem.

Honestly the most useful thing about CTF competition is that it is just you and the problem. 
No documentation to fall back on, no teammate to ask, no time to go deep on Google. You 
either know it or you figure it out fast. Placing in the 87th percentile of the experienced 
bracket was a good signal that six years in the industry built something real, but the misses 
showed me exactly what to work on next.
